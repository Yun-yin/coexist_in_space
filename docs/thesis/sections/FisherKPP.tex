\section{Fisher-KPP Equation}

Here we do some basic analysis on the Fisher-KPP model \citep{fisher_wave_1937}. The Fisher-KPP model is written as,
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}
    =
    D \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}
    +
    r u (1-au)
\end{equation*}
Introduce the nondimensional variables
\begin{align*}
    u' = au,\qquad  \tau = rt,  \qquad  z = \sqrt{\frac{r}{D}}\,x.
\end{align*}
Then the equation becomes
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial u'}{\partial \tau}
    =
    \frac{\partial^2 u'}{\partial z^2}
    +
    u'(1-u').
\end{equation*}
Therefore, it is sufficient to study the normalized Fisher equation
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}
    =
    \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}
    +
    u(1-u).
\end{equation*}

We look for travelling wave solutions whose profile remains unchanged during propagation. Let
\begin{equation*}
    z = x-ct,
\end{equation*}
where $c$ is the wave speed. Substituting $u(x,t)=u(z)$ into the PDE yields
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\mathrm{d}^2u}{\mathrm{d}z^2}
    +
    c\frac{\mathrm{d}u}{\mathrm{d}z}
    +
    u(1-u)
    =
    0.
\end{equation*}
The nonlinear term
\begin{equation*}
    f(u)=u(1-u)
\end{equation*}
has two equilibria, $u=0$ and $u=1$ (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher_allen}A). The travelling wave solution connects these two states. Near $u=0$, we linearize the equation by assuming
\begin{equation*}
    u(z)\sim e^{\lambda z}.
\end{equation*}
Substitution gives the characteristic equation
\begin{equation*}
    \lambda^2 + c\lambda + 1 = 0.
\end{equation*}
Hence,
\begin{equation*}
    \lambda
=
\frac{-c \pm \sqrt{c^2-4}}{2}.
\end{equation*}
To ensure decay as $z\to+\infty$, we require $\lambda<0$, which implies
\begin{equation*}
    c \ge 2.
\end{equation*}

We now study the travelling wave equation on the phase plane. Define
\begin{equation*}
    p=\frac{\mathrm{d}u}{\mathrm{d}z}.
\end{equation*}
Then the second-order ODE can be rewritten as
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
    \dot{u} = p,\\
    \dot{p} = -cp-u(1-u),
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}
where the dot denotes differentiation with respect to $z$.
The equilibria are
\begin{equation*}
    (u^*,p^*)=(0,0),\qquad (1,0).
\end{equation*}

The Jacobian matrix is
\begin{equation*}
\mathbf{J}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1\\
2u-1 & -c
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation*}
At $(0,0)$,
\begin{equation*}
\mathbf{J}|_{(0,0)}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1\\
-1 & -c
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation*}
whose characteristic equation is
\begin{equation*}
\lambda^2 + c\lambda + 1 = 0.
\end{equation*}
For $c\ge2$, both eigenvalues are real and negative, so $(0,0)$ is a stable node.
At $(1,0)$,
\begin{equation*}
\mathbf{J}|_{(1,0)}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1\\
1 & -c
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation*}
whose characteristic equation is
\begin{equation*}
\lambda^2 + c\lambda - 1 = 0.
\end{equation*}
The eigenvalues are
\begin{equation*}
\lambda
=
\frac{-c \pm \sqrt{c^2+4}}{2},
\end{equation*}
which have opposite signs. Therefore, $(1,0)$ is a saddle point.

The travelling wave corresponds to a heteroclinic trajectory connecting $(1,0)$ to $(0,0)$ in the phase plane (Figure~\ref{fig:trajectory}). Let this trajectory be written as
\begin{equation*}
p=\varphi(u).
\end{equation*}
Since
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\mathrm{d}u}{\mathrm{d}z}=\varphi(u),
\end{equation*}
we obtain
\begin{equation*}
    z-z_0
    =
    \int_{u_0}^{u}
    \frac{1}{\varphi(u)}
    \,\mathrm{d}u.
\end{equation*}

Thus the travelling wave profile $u(z)$ can be reconstructed from the phase trajectory.

Consequently, the travelling wave solution satisfies the boundary conditions
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{z\to-\infty}u(z)=1,
\qquad
\lim_{z\to+\infty}u(z)=0.
\end{equation*}

To approximate the wave profile, we consider the large-speed limit $c\to\infty$. Introduce the stretched coordinate
\begin{equation*}
\xi = \frac{z}{c}.
\end{equation*}
The travelling wave equation becomes
\begin{equation*}
\frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\mathrm{d}^2u}{\mathrm{d}\xi^2}
+
\frac{\mathrm{d}u}{\mathrm{d}\xi}
+
u(1-u)
=
0.
\end{equation*}
Assume an asymptotic expansion of the form
\begin{equation*}
u(\xi)
=
u_0(\xi)
+
\frac{1}{c^2}u_1(\xi)
+
\mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{1}{c^4}\right).
\end{equation*}
Collecting terms of equal order in $c^{-1}$ gives
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\mathrm{d}u_0}{\mathrm{d}\xi}
+
u_0(1-u_0)
=
0,
\end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\mathrm{d}^2u_0}{\mathrm{d}\xi^2}
+
\frac{\mathrm{d}u_1}{\mathrm{d}\xi}
+
u_1(1-2u_0)
=
0.
\end{equation*}
Imposing the symmetry condition
\begin{equation*}
u(0)=\frac12,
\end{equation*}
we obtain
\begin{equation*}
u_0(z)
=
\frac{1}{1+e^{z/c}},
\end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*}
u_1(z)
=
\frac{e^{z/c}}
{\left(1+e^{z/c}\right)^2}
\ln\left(
\frac{4e^{z/c}}
{\left(1+e^{z/c}\right)^2}
\right).
\end{equation*}
Therefore,
\begin{equation*}
u(z)
\approx
u_0(z)
+
\frac{1}{c^2}u_1(z).
\end{equation*}
This approximation remains accurate even for the minimal wave speed $c=2$.
