\section{Introduction}

Understanding biodiversity is one of the central goals 
of modern ecology \citep{pimm_complexity_1984,mccann_diversitystability_2000,sutherland_identification_2013}. Explaining why so many species coexist,and how such coexistence can persist over long time scales, remains a fundamental problem in biodiversity research \citep{may_theoretical_2007}. Historically, species-rich ecosystems were often regarded as natural and self-evident, since diverse and apparently stable ecological communities are commonly observed in nature \citep{mccann_diversitystability_2000}. Biodiversity was therefore traditionally viewed as an intrinsic property of ecological systems that contributes to the maintenance of ecosystem functioning and stability \citep{clements_nature_1936}.

Some of earlier empirical research has further suggested that highly diverse ecosystems tend to exhibit greater functional complexity and ecological resilience \citep{pimentel_animal_1961,hairston_relationship_1968}. This observation led to the classical hypothesis that biodiversity promotes ecosystem stability by increasing the capacity of communities to maintain species coexistence under environmental fluctuations and perturbations \citep{elton_ecology_1977,pimm_complexity_1984}. 
However, despite extensive empirical and theoretical work, the precise relationship between biodiversity and stability remains controversial \citep{hooper_effects_2005}. One major difficulty arises from the fact that both ``biodiversity'' and ``stability'' have been defined in many different and sometimes incompatible ways \citep{grimm_babel_1997, dominguez-garcia_unveiling_2019}, resulting in a lack of unified theoretical predictions and conclusive empirical evidence.

In contrast to purely descriptive perspectives, ecological communities can also be understood as dynamical systems governed by species interactions. With the development of complex systems theory and random matrix theory, \citet{may_will_1972} first proposed that large ecological systems may in fact be intrinsically difficult to stabilize when species interactions are sufficiently complex and effectively random. By linearizing the dynamics around a stationary equilibrium, the local stability of the system can be determined from the eigenvalues of the interaction matrix. Random matrix theory \citep{pastur_spectrum_1972,tao_random_2010} predicts that, for sufficiently large random systems, the eigenvalue distribution converges toward a circular region in the complex plane whose radius increases with system size. Consequently, as the number of interacting species increases, the probability that all eigenvalues remain within the stable region decreases. This result suggests that increasing complexity may destabilize ecological communities rather than stabilize them. 

As a consequence, stability cannot simply be regarded as an automatic property of large ecological systems. Instead, species coexistence must be understood from the specific structural and dynamical properties that distinguish ecological communities from purely random systems. One major research direction therefore focuses on the structure of species interactions, namely the properties of the interaction matrix governing community dynamics. The type of species interaction plays a critical role in determining stability because it constrains the sign structure of the interaction matrix. Previous studies have shown that predator--prey communities are generally more likely to remain stable than purely competitive or mutualistic systems of comparable size \citep{allesina_stability_2012}. This difference arises because interaction matrices with opposite-signed symmetric elements tend to produce eigenvalue distributions confined within narrower regions of the complex plane, thereby increasing the probability of local stability. In addition to interaction type, the topological structure of ecological networks also strongly influences community stability \citep{bascompte_plant-animal_2007, spiesman_habitat_2013,grass_past_2018}. Network properties such as nestedness \citep{bascompte_nested_2003, mariani_nestedness_2019}, modularity \citep{thebault_stability_2010,sales-pardo_importance_2017}, and characteristic interaction motifs \citep{klaise_origin_2017} determine the connectivity patterns and long-range organization of ecological communities\citep{letten_mechanistic_2019}. These structural features can substantially alter the propagation of perturbations through the network and therefore influence the persistence of species coexistence. Empirical studies further suggest that highly modular structures may promote coexistence by compartmentalizing disturbances, whereas strong nestedness is often associated with reduced dynamical stability \citep{bascompte_plant-animal_2007, thebault_stability_2010}.

Another important line of research focuses on the functional form of species interactions and population growth dynamics. Ecological interactions are highly nonlinear processes and may not always be adequately captured by the generalized Lotka--Volterra (GLV) framework \citep{case_illustrated_2000, allesina_stability_2012, barabas_chessons_2018}. Consequently, more realistic nonlinear functional responses, such as Holling-type interaction functions \citep{holling_characteristics_1959}, have been incorporated into community dynamical models. Studies have suggested that these nonlinear interaction structures can substantially increase the probability of stability even in large random ecological systems \citep{rohr_structural_2014, hatton_diversity_2024}.

However, beyond the dynamics of local communities, spatial structure also plays a fundamental role in maintaining species coexistence \citep{levin_dispersion_1974}. Classical island biogeography theory first predicted that larger islands or habitat patches generally support more species than smaller ones \citep{macarthur_theory_2001}, a pattern that has since been confirmed across a wide range of ecosystems (Figure~\ref{fig:intro}A). Building upon this observation, neutral theory explained large-scale biodiversity patterns by assuming ecological equivalence among species \citep{hubbell_unified_2001,hubbell_neutral_2005}, namely that species are effectively identical in their demographic properties, dispersal abilities, and interactions. Under these assumptions, neutral models successfully reproduced several large-scale biodiversity patterns, including species abundance distributions.

More recently, theoretical studies have further demonstrated that sufficiently large spatial systems composed of many habitat patches may maintain long-term coexistence even when local exclusion occurs within individual patches \citep{hanski_metapopulation_2000,loreau_meta-ecosystems_2003,servan_coexistence_2018,miller_coexistence_2024} (Figure~\ref{fig:intro}B). In contrast, niche theory emphasizes that larger spatial regions are more likely to contain heterogeneous environments and therefore provide a greater diversity of ecological niches\citep{amarasekare_competitive_2003,girardin_effect_2019, guimaraes_structure_2020}. From this perspective, large-scale biodiversity emerges primarily through species turnover along environmental gradients. The concept of $\beta$-diversity was subsequently introduced to quantify the magnitude of such compositional turnover across space \citep{wang_ecosystem_2014}.



Despite their differences, both neutral and niche-based perspectives ultimately highlight the importance of dispersal processes in shaping biodiversity patterns \citep{holmes_partial_1994,amarasekare_competitive_2003}. Whether dispersal occurs randomly or directionally, the movement of individuals through space fundamentally links local community dynamics to regional biodiversity patterns \citep{gurtin_mathematical_1974,gude_bacterial_2020}.



Reaction--diffusion models provide a natural mathematical framework for coupling species interactions with spatial dispersal. Originally developed to explain biological pattern formation during development \citep{turing_chemical_1990}, reaction--diffusion systems are now widely used in mathematical biology to describe the combined effects of local interactions and spatial movement among particles, biomolecules, or ecological populations \citep{kondo_reaction-diffusion_2010}. In community ecology, the reaction term typically represents local species interactions, whereas the diffusion term describes dispersal processes.

A central topic in reaction--diffusion theory concerns traveling wave solutions \citep{fife_approach_1977,gardner_existence_1982, hou_traveling_2008, wang_existence_2024}, which describe propagating interfaces connecting alternative stable states. Ecologically, these fronts may represent transitions between regions dominated by different species or communities (Figure~\ref{fig:intro}C). In particular, stationary traveling waves with zero propagation speed correspond to stable spatial interfaces \citep{tuckwell_solitons_1979}, implying long-term species turnover without requiring explicit environmental gradients \citep{namba_spatial_1980,carrere_spreading_2018}. Such structures therefore provide a potential mechanism for large-scale coexistence and spatial $\beta$-diversity generated purely through endogenous ecological dynamics.

The one-species case has been extensively studied and is often interpreted as the invasion or colonization of empty habitat \citep{holmes_partial_1994}. Two-species systems have also received substantial attention because interspecific interactions can generate considerably richer spatial dynamics \citep{pao_coexistence_1981, carrere_spreading_2018,ma_speed_2019,wang_asymptotic_2022}. Nevertheless, despite being the simplest interacting ecological systems, the existence and stability of traveling wave solutions in competitive two-species models are still not fully understood \citep{hou_traveling_2008}. Extending these analyses to multispecies communities is substantially more challenging because the dimensionality and complexity of the system increase rapidly with species richness.

\newpage

In this thesis, we investigate the properties of stationary traveling wave solutions with zero propagation speed in ecological reaction--diffusion systems. Using simplified two-species and multispecies models, we analyze both analytically and numerically the spatial structure of transition fronts connecting alternative community states. In particular, we focus on the conditions under which stable coexistence interfaces can emerge in the absence of explicit environmental gradients. Our results show that, under bistable interaction structures, the resulting spatial transition zones can often be approximated by logistic-like functions. Extending from the two-species case to simplified multispecies communities, we further demonstrate that dispersal and species interactions alone may generate persistent spatial turnover and heterogeneous community composition at large spatial scales. Based on these spatial population distributions, we additionally propose a continuous measure of $\beta$-diversity derived from the equilibrium abundance profiles. This framework provides a quantitative description of species turnover generated intrinsically by ecological dynamics in spatially homogeneous, or approximately neutral, environments.
